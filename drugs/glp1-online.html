Frequently Asked Questions About Getting GLP-1 Drugs Online

Is it legal to get GLP-1 drugs online?

Yes, it is legal to receive a GLP-1 prescription through a licensed telehealth provider in the United States. These platforms employ board-certified physicians or licensed nurse practitioners who evaluate your eligibility, review your medical history, and issue a valid prescription if appropriate. The prescription is filled by a licensed pharmacy — either a retail pharmacy or a compounding pharmacy depending on the drug.

Regulated telehealth prescribing of GLP-1 medications is fully compliant with DEA and state medical board rules, as GLP-1 agonists are not controlled substances. Always use a licensed, US-based platform with licensed providers.

What's the difference between branded and compounded semaglutide?

Branded semaglutide products like Ozempic and Wegovy are FDA-approved medications manufactured by Novo Nordisk and dispensed in fixed formulations. Compounded semaglutide is mixed by a licensed 503A or 503B compounding pharmacy and may be prescribed when a branded drug is unavailable or when a physician determines compounding is medically appropriate.

Compounded versions are not FDA-approved, meaning there is no independent review of their purity, potency, or stability. The FDA has noted safety concerns about some compounded GLP-1 preparations. Patients should discuss risks and benefits with their prescriber before choosing compounded alternatives.

Do I need insurance to get Ozempic online?

No, you do not need insurance to get an Ozempic prescription through a telehealth provider. Platforms like Hims & Hers, Ro, and others operate on a cash-pay model. You pay a monthly subscription or fee that covers the provider consultation and, in many cases, the medication itself. Costs typically range from $145 to $449 per month depending on the platform and the specific GLP-1 drug or compounded equivalent prescribed.

Keep in mind that most telehealth GLP-1 services prescribe compounded semaglutide rather than branded Ozempic. You can also check manufacturer copay cards on SaveRx.ai for the official Ozempic savings program.

How much does it cost to get Wegovy online without insurance?

The cash-pay cost to get Wegovy or a semaglutide equivalent online without insurance typically ranges from $145 to $449 per month depending on the platform, dose, and whether the medication is branded or compounded. Ro Body currently offers semaglutide starting at $145/month. Hims & Hers starts at $199/month.

These costs are generally much lower than filling branded Wegovy at a retail pharmacy, where the list price is over $1,300/month before coupons or insurance. If you're eligible for the manufacturer's savings card, check SaveRx's Wegovy savings page for the official program.

What GLP-1 drugs can I get online?

Online telehealth providers can prescribe a range of GLP-1 receptor agonists. Most commonly, platforms prescribe semaglutide (the active ingredient in Ozempic and Wegovy) and tirzepatide (the active ingredient in Mounjaro and Zepbound). Depending on the platform and your state, you may also be able to get liraglutide (Saxenda/Victoza) or oral semaglutide (Rybelsus).

Many platforms prescribe compounded versions of these drugs, which can be significantly cheaper than branded versions. Always ask your provider whether they're prescribing a branded or compounded product and from which pharmacy it will be dispensed.

How long does it take to get a GLP-1 prescription online?

The timeline varies by platform. Most telehealth providers process your intake form and connect you with a provider within 24–72 hours. After your provider visit (typically asynchronous — via form — or a brief video call), a prescription is sent to a pharmacy if you're approved. Medication delivery usually takes an additional 3–5 business days.

Some platforms like Hims & Hers advertise a 2–3 day turnaround from consultation to prescription. Total time from signup to receiving your medication is typically 5–10 business days, though this can vary depending on your state and the pharmacy used.

Is compounded semaglutide safe?

Compounded semaglutide can be safe when prepared by a licensed, FDA-registered 503B outsourcing facility or 503A compounding pharmacy following proper quality controls. However, because compounded drugs are not FDA-approved, there is variability in quality between compounding facilities.

The FDA has issued warnings about some compounded semaglutide products citing concerns about potency, purity, and improper dosing instructions. If you choose compounded semaglutide, ask your provider which pharmacy it comes from and verify it is accredited (e.g., PCAB-accredited). When branded GLP-1 medications are available and affordable via a savings program, they remain the safest option.
