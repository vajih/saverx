What is Repatha?

Repatha® (evolocumab) is a prescription PCSK9 inhibitor manufactured by Amgen — an injectable biologic that lowers LDL ("bad") cholesterol by up to 60% on top of statin therapy. If you've been on the maximum statin and your LDL is still too high, Repatha is typically the next step your cardiologist will consider.

Who uses Repatha?

  • Adults with high LDL not controlled by statins alone — As an add-on to diet and statin therapy when LDL remains too high.
  • Familial hypercholesterolemia (FH) patients — Both heterozygous (HeFH) and homozygous (HoFH) forms. The 420mg monthly dose is specifically FDA-approved for HoFH.
  • Adults with established cardiovascular disease (CVD) — Repatha is approved to reduce the risk of heart attack, stroke, and coronary revascularization in adults who have had a prior cardiovascular event. In the landmark FOURIER trial (27,564 patients), Repatha reduced major cardiovascular events by 15% vs. placebo when added to statin therapy.
  • Statin-intolerant patients — If you cannot tolerate statins due to muscle pain or other side effects and your LDL remains dangerously high, Repatha may be an option.

You administer Repatha yourself at home via a subcutaneous injection using a prefilled SureClick® autoinjector or syringe. The most common dose is 140 mg every 2 weeks or 420 mg once monthly (given as three consecutive 140 mg injections).

What Does Repatha Cost Without Insurance?

One of the most common questions we hear is: "How much does Repatha actually cost?" The short answer: a lot — unless you use a savings program. If you're paying full price, you may be spending hundreds per month that you don't have to. Here's what you're looking at without any assistance:

Monthly (2 autoinjectors, 140 mg/mL each) $620 – $700

Why is Repatha so expensive?

  • It's a biologic: Unlike small-molecule pills, Repatha is a laboratory-engineered monoclonal antibody. Manufacturing biologics is complex, tightly regulated, and significantly more costly than making traditional drugs.
  • No generic or biosimilar available: As of 2026, no FDA-approved biosimilar for evolocumab exists in the U.S. Repatha has no price competition, and most patients pay for the brand at whatever the formulary dictates.
  • Specialty tier placement: Insurance plans typically place Repatha on Tier 4 or 5 (specialty), meaning your coinsurance or copay can be hundreds of dollars per month without assistance.
  • Prior authorization burden: PA is almost universally required, which can delay access by weeks. Prior authorization delays are one of the most common reasons patients end up paying out-of-pocket before their savings card can even be applied.

The good news: The majority of commercially insured patients who apply do qualify — and can reduce their monthly cost to just $5 using the official Amgen copay program. If you have no insurance, the Patient Assistance Program may cover the full cost. Not sure which path fits you? The REPATHA SUPPORT™ Copay Card

Amgen offers the REPATHA SUPPORT™ Enrollment Program, which includes a copay card that dramatically reduces your monthly cost if you have commercial (private) health insurance.

What you can save

  • Pay as little as $5 per month for your Repatha prescription.
  • The program covers up to $3,600 per year in out-of-pocket costs (approximately $300/month in maximum benefit).
  • There is no limit on the number of times you can use it within the benefit year, up to the annual maximum.

Who qualifies?

  • You must have commercial health insurance (employer-sponsored, individual marketplace, or similar private plan).
  • Your insurance plan must cover Repatha — the copay card bridges your out-of-pocket cost, but is not a substitute for insurance coverage.
  • You must be a U.S. resident and meet the program's terms and conditions.
  • You must not be enrolled in Medicare, Medicaid, CHIP, TRICARE, or any other government-funded health program.

Important restriction: Federal law prohibits the use of manufacturer copay assistance cards with Medicare, Medicaid, or other government programs. Using the card while enrolled in a government plan is a federal healthcare violation. See the Medicare and uninsured options section below for legal alternatives.

Key program details

  • The savings card must be renewed annually. Your enrollment does not automatically carry over to the next benefit year.
  • The copay card applies to your out-of-pocket cost (copay, coinsurance, or deductible) as billed by the pharmacy — it does not affect what your insurance pays.
  • Not valid if Repatha is not covered by your insurance plan. If your plan does not cover Repatha, you would need to pay the full retail price and the card cannot be applied to that.
  • Available at specialty pharmacies only — these are pharmacies that handle high-cost injectable and biologic medications (like Accredo, CVS Specialty, or Walgreens Specialty). Repatha cannot be filled at a standard retail pharmacy counter.

Worth knowing: Most specialty pharmacies will not automatically apply your copay card. You need to provide the card number proactively at each fill and confirm it was processed before completing the transaction.

Step-by-Step: How to Get the Repatha Copay Card

Follow these steps in order. Skipping steps — especially prior authorization — is the most common reason patients pay full price.

  1. Confirm you have commercial insurance — not Medicare or Medicaid

    Check your insurance card. If it says Medicare, Medicaid, or any state/federal program, skip to the Medicare & Uninsured Options section below. The copay card only works with private commercial insurance.

  2. Get your Repatha prescription and prior authorization started

    Ask your cardiologist or primary care doctor to send a Repatha prescription to your specialty pharmacy and to submit a prior authorization (PA) to your insurance. The PA requires documentation that statins were tried and either failed to adequately lower your LDL, or that you could not tolerate them. Without the PA, your insurance will likely deny coverage. This process typically takes 1–3 weeks — delays are normal, not a sign something went wrong. Ask your doctor for starter samples while you wait so you can begin treatment immediately.

  3. Enroll in the REPATHA SUPPORT™ program

    Visit repatha.com/patient/savings-and-support or call 1-844-REPATHA (1-844-737-2842). Have your insurance card handy. Complete the enrollment form — you will need your name, date of birth, insurance information, and prescribing doctor's name. You can also have your doctor's office enroll you on your behalf.

  4. Download, print, or save your savings card

    After enrollment is confirmed, Amgen will provide your savings card digitally (email or download) or by mail. You can also show the card directly on your phone at most specialty pharmacies. Save the card number and expiration date somewhere accessible.

  5. Contact your specialty pharmacy before your first fill

    Repatha is typically dispensed through specialty pharmacies such as Accredo, CVS Specialty, or Walgreens Specialty Pharmacy. Call the pharmacy directly to (a) confirm they accept the REPATHA SUPPORT™ copay card and (b) confirm your PA has been approved by insurance before they ship. This prevents getting surprised by a $600+ bill at your door.

  6. Provide the card at each fill and confirm your $5 charge

    Every time you fill your prescription, provide your copay card number. Your charge should be $5 or as close to $0 as your card benefit allows. Ask the pharmacist to confirm the card was applied before completing the transaction. If the total seems wrong, do not pay — ask them to rerun the card or call Amgen support directly.

  7. Renew your card annually before it expires

    Set a calendar reminder one month before your card expiration. Renewal requires re-confirming eligibility but is usually fast. If you miss renewal and fill without a valid card, you will be charged the full insurance out-of-pocket cost.

Medicare, Medicaid & Uninsured: Your Options

If the REPATHA SUPPORT™ copay card is not available to you, here is exactly what to do instead. These are real, working alternatives — not consolation prizes.

Quick reference — if you have Medicare:

① Skip the copay card (federal law prohibits it) → ② Look into the Patient Assistance Program or the $2,000 Part D out-of-pocket cap → ③ Gather income documentation (tax return or SSA letter) → ④ Ask your doctor's office to help with the application → ⑤ Expect approval in 5–15 business days

Why the copay card does NOT work with Medicare

This is probably the most common question we get. The answer is simple but important: federal anti-kickback law (42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7b) makes it illegal for drug manufacturers to offer copay subsidies to patients enrolled in any government health program — including Medicare Part D, Medicaid, CHIP, TRICARE, and VA benefits.

This is not an Amgen policy choice. Every manufacturer copay card for every drug has this same restriction. It exists because the government negotiates its own drug prices and does not allow manufacturers to influence patient behavior through copay subsidies. Using a copay card while on Medicare — even accidentally — is considered a federal healthcare violation.

What this means for you: If you turned 65 and switched to Medicare, or if you have dual coverage (Medicare + commercial), the copay card is off the table. But the alternatives below can be equally effective — especially the $2,000 annual out-of-pocket cap and the Patient Assistance Program. For a full breakdown, see copay cards vs patient assistance programs.

Medicare Part D patients: your action plan

  • $2,000 annual out-of-pocket cap (Inflation Reduction Act): Since 2025, Medicare Part D caps your total drug out-of-pocket costs at $2,000 per year. For a drug like Repatha, this means you may hit the cap in the first few months — and pay $0 for the rest of the year. Ask your plan: "When will I reach my Part D out-of-pocket cap based on my current prescriptions?"
  • Medicare Extra Help (Low Income Subsidy / LIS): If your income is below ~$22,590/year (single) or ~$30,660/year (married), this federal program can reduce your Part D premiums, deductibles, and copays to near-zero. Apply through Social Security (ssa.gov) or call 1-800-772-1213. Processing takes 2–4 weeks.
  • Medicare Savings Programs (MSPs): State-run programs that help with Medicare premiums and cost-sharing. Your free State Health Insurance Assistance Program (SHIP) counselor can walk you through what you qualify for. Find yours at shiphelp.org.
  • Switch Part D plans during open enrollment: PCSK9 inhibitor coverage varies dramatically between Part D plans. During open enrollment (October 15 – December 7), compare plans at medicare.gov/plan-compare. Enter "evolocumab" and compare total annual cost — not just monthly premium. Patients who switch to a plan with better Repatha tier placement can save hundreds per year.

Uninsured or underinsured patients: your action plan

REPATHA SUPPORT™ Patient Assistance Program (PAP): Amgen provides Repatha at no cost to qualifying patients. Eligibility typically extends to patients earning up to 600% of the federal poverty level (~$94,000/year for a single person in 2026). This is not a discount — it is free medication shipped directly to you.

How to apply for the PAP (what actually happens)

  1. Download the application at amgen.com/patients/patient-assistance or call 1-844-737-2842 and request one.
  2. Gather required documents: proof of income (most recent tax return, W-2, or Social Security benefit statement), proof of U.S. residency, and evidence that you are uninsured or underinsured (denial letter from insurer, or confirmation of no coverage).
  3. Your doctor completes their section of the application — including the prescription details and a medical justification statement. Most cardiology offices are familiar with this process.
  4. Submit the application by fax, mail, or through the online portal. Amgen typically processes applications within 5–10 business days. If approved, medication is shipped to your doctor's office or directly to you.
  5. Renewal is required annually. Amgen will send a renewal notice — but set your own reminder 60 days before expiration.

What to Do at the Pharmacy (If Something Goes Wrong)

Even when you've done everything right, things can still go sideways at the pharmacy. If your price is still high after using a savings option, it usually comes down to one of three issues: the copay card wasn't applied, a prior authorization is missing or pending, or your insurance denied coverage. Each one is fixable — here's exactly how.

If the price is still high ($100+ instead of $5)

This usually means the copay card was not applied to your transaction. Ask the pharmacist: "Can you reprocess this fill with my manufacturer copay card?" Give them the card number, BIN, PCN, and group number directly. Most pharmacists can re-run the claim in minutes. If they say the card was already applied, ask them to read back the exact amount the card covered — sometimes the card processes but hits an annual cap you didn't know about.

If the copay card is rejected

The most common reason is a prior authorization issue on the insurance side, not a problem with the card itself. Ask the pharmacist: "Is my insurance showing an approved PA for this drug?" If the PA expired or was never approved, the copay card cannot process because there's no insurance claim to apply it to. Your next step is to call your doctor's office and ask them to resubmit the PA — and to call Amgen support at 1-844-737-2842 for help expediting it.

If the pharmacist is unsure how to process the card

This happens more often than you'd expect, especially at smaller specialty pharmacies or when there's been staff turnover. Ask the pharmacist to call the REPATHA SUPPORT™ pharmacy help line at 1-844-737-2842. Amgen has a dedicated team that walks pharmacists through the technical billing process. You should not have to solve this yourself.

If your insurance denies coverage entirely

This is a different situation from a PA delay. If your insurance plan does not cover Repatha — or actively excludes PCSK9 inhibitors — the copay card cannot be used because there is no insurance claim to apply it to. Your options:

  • Ask your doctor to submit a formulary exception request — a formal appeal asking your plan to cover Repatha despite it not being on their formulary. Include a letter of medical necessity.
  • If denied on appeal, consider the Patient Assistance Program (see Medicare & Uninsured Options above).
  • During open enrollment, switch to an insurance plan that covers PCSK9 inhibitors. Check formularies before switching.

If you already paid full price

Don't assume you're stuck. Call the specialty pharmacy and ask: "Can you reverse and rebill my last Repatha fill with my copay card?" Most pharmacies can do this within 14 days of the original fill. If the pharmacy cannot reverse it, call Amgen directly — they sometimes offer retroactive reimbursement for eligible patients who had a valid card at the time of fill.

Common Mistakes (and How to Avoid Them)

Many patients assume the process will "just work" once they have a prescription. In reality, small missteps are the most common reason patients pay far more than they should — or give up on treatment entirely. The good news: every mistake below is avoidable, and insurance denials are often reversed on appeal.

  • Trying to fill Repatha at a retail pharmacy.
    Repatha is a specialty drug. Standard retail pharmacies (CVS, Walgreens, Rite Aid) almost never carry it. You must fill through a specialty pharmacy — and confirm they accept your copay card before you fill. Calling ahead is not optional.
  • Filling before the prior authorization is approved.
    If you fill Repatha before your insurance approves the PA, you will almost certainly be charged the full cash price. Wait for written or verbal PA approval from your pharmacy or insurer before filling.
  • Using the copay card with Medicare or Medicaid.
    This is a federal compliance issue — it cannot be used, period. If you are on Medicare, see the Medicare options above. There are legitimate alternatives.
  • Letting the enrollment card expire without renewing.
    Annual renewal is required. Many patients discover mid-year that their card expired months ago and they have been overpaying. Set a reminder at least 30 days before expiration.
  • Assuming the copay card works if Repatha is not covered by your plan.
    The copay card reduces your out-of-pocket portion of a covered drug. If Repatha is not on your formulary or is specifically excluded, the copay card cannot be applied. Your first step is always to confirm coverage.
  • Not appealing after an insurance denial.
    PA denials are common on first submission. You have the right to appeal, and appeals often succeed — especially with a letter of medical necessity from your cardiologist. Do not accept a denial as final before pursuing at least one appeal.
  • Ignoring copay accumulator policies.
    Some insurance plans use "copay accumulators" — meaning the money your copay card pays does not count toward your deductible or out-of-pocket maximum. You could use the card all year and still owe your full deductible. Call your insurer and ask: "Does my plan use a copay accumulator program?" Learn more in our copay accumulator explainer.

Pro Tips to Maximize Your Savings

  • Ask your cardiologist for a free starter sample.
    While your PA is being processed, ask your doctor if they have Repatha starter samples. Amgen provides starter sample kits to prescribing physicians. This can keep you on therapy for free during the 1–4 week PA window.
  • Enroll in REPATHA SUPPORT™ before your first fill — not after.
    Enrollment is instant online. Do it the same day your doctor sends the prescription, so the card is ready when the PA approves. There's no reason to wait.
  • Use Amgen's hub services for PA support.
    REPATHA SUPPORT™ includes a team that can help you navigate prior authorization and insurance appeals. Call 1-844-737-2842 and ask for PA assistance. This is a free service that can save enormous time.
  • Compare specialty pharmacies for convenience and reliability.
    Accredo, CVS Specialty, and Walgreens Specialty all dispense Repatha and accept the copay card. Some ship directly to your home with cold packing. Ask your doctor which specialty pharmacy is in-network for your plan to avoid extra fees.
  • If your plan changes at open enrollment, re-check your PA and card.
    Switching insurance plans can invalidate your prior authorization and your copay card enrollment simultaneously. Do it right: before switching plans, confirm your new plan covers Repatha, that a PA submission is ready, and re-enroll in the savings program under your new insurance.
  • Track your annual benefit usage.
    The copay card has a $3,600/year maximum. If you are approaching the cap (unlikely at $5/fill, but possible if your plan's cost-sharing is high), contact Amgen to explore additional support options before the benefit runs out.

Repatha Savings: Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use the Repatha copay card with Medicare?

No. The REPATHA SUPPORT™ copay card cannot be used by anyone enrolled in Medicare, Medicaid, CHIP, TRICARE, or any other federal or state government health program. This is not a policy choice by Amgen — it is a requirement under federal anti-kickback law.

If you are on Medicare, your options include Medicare Part D Extra Help (Low Income Subsidy), Medicare Savings Programs, annual plan comparisons at medicare.gov, and the $2,000 annual out-of-pocket cap under the Inflation Reduction Act starting in 2025.

How much can I save with the Repatha copay card?

Eligible commercially insured patients may pay as little as $5 per month. The program covers up to $3,600 per year in out-of-pocket costs — which works out to up to $300/month in benefit. Your actual savings depend on your plan's cost-sharing for Repatha and whether the copay card fully covers your portion.

For most patients with commercial insurance that covers Repatha, the $5/month rate is achievable.

What if I have no insurance and cannot afford Repatha?

Amgen offers the REPATHA SUPPORT™ Patient Assistance Program for qualifying uninsured or underinsured patients. Eligible patients may receive Repatha at no cost. Income limits apply and are reviewed based on the federal poverty level.

You can apply at amgen.com/patients/patient-assistance or by calling 1-844-737-2842. Your doctor will need to sign off on the application. Additional resources include NeedyMeds.org and RxAssist.org.

Does Repatha require prior authorization from my insurance?

Yes — nearly universally. Most insurance plans require prior authorization before covering Repatha. The PA process typically requires your doctor to document that:

  • You have been diagnosed with hyperlipidemia, familial hypercholesterolemia, or established cardiovascular disease.
  • You have tried and failed to achieve adequate LDL reduction on maximally tolerated statin therapy (or cannot tolerate statins).

The PA review process usually takes 1–3 weeks. If denied, you have the right to appeal. Amgen's REPATHA SUPPORT™ team can assist with PA support — call 1-844-737-2842.

Where do I fill my Repatha prescription?

Repatha is a specialty medication and is dispensed through specialty pharmacies only — not typical retail or grocery pharmacies. Common options include:

  • Accredo (Express Scripts' specialty pharmacy) — 1-800-803-2523
  • CVS Specialty Pharmacy — 1-800-237-2767
  • Walgreens Specialty Pharmacy — 1-866-973-7503
  • Your insurer's preferred in-network specialty pharmacy (check your plan documents for the lowest cost option)

Most specialty pharmacies ship Repatha directly to your home with proper cold packing (Repatha must be refrigerated). Always confirm copay card acceptance with the pharmacy before first fill.

How often do I need to renew the Repatha savings card?

The REPATHA SUPPORT™ copay card must be renewed annually. Your enrollment does not automatically carry over from one benefit year to the next. You will typically receive a renewal reminder from Amgen, but it is wise to set your own calendar reminder 30 days before expiration.

Renewal is usually straightforward — you will need to re-confirm your insurance eligibility and agree to updated program terms. Call 1-844-737-2842 or renew online at repatha.com/patient/savings-and-support.

Can I use the Repatha copay card if my insurance requires step therapy?

Step therapy requirements are a separate matter from the copay card. If your plan requires you to first try (and fail on) a statin or another cholesterol drug before approving Repatha, you would need to complete that process — or your doctor would need to document a compelling clinical exception — before Repatha would be covered.

The copay card applies only once your insurance covers Repatha. If coverage is denied due to step therapy, work with your doctor to either fulfill the step therapy requirement or request a step therapy exception based on your medical history.

Is there a generic version of Repatha?

No. There is currently no generic version of Repatha (evolocumab). As a biologic, Repatha cannot have a traditional generic — only a biosimilar, which undergoes a different regulatory approval pathway. As of 2026, no FDA-approved biosimilar for evolocumab is available in the United States.

The closest alternative is Praluent® (alirocumab), the other PCSK9 inhibitor. Praluent is not a generic or biosimilar of Repatha — it is a different drug in the same class. Your cardiologist can advise whether switching is appropriate. See our Praluent savings guide for that drug's copay programs.

What if my insurance denies Repatha after prior authorization?

A denial is not the end of the road. You have the right to appeal — and appeals frequently succeed, especially with strong clinical documentation. Here is the typical process:

  • Step 1: Ask your doctor to submit a formal appeal with a letter of medical necessity. This should document your LDL levels, statin history (tried and failed or intolerant), and cardiovascular risk factors.
  • Step 2: If the first-level appeal is denied, request an external review — an independent third party evaluates the decision. Most states require insurers to offer this option.
  • Step 3: Contact Amgen's REPATHA SUPPORT™ team at 1-844-737-2842 — they have experienced staff who help with insurance appeals and know the documentation requirements inside and out.

If all else fails, consider the Patient Assistance Program (for uninsured/underinsured patients) or switching to a plan that covers PCSK9 inhibitors during open enrollment.

Can my doctor's office help me apply for savings programs?

Yes — and in many cases, they already know how. Cardiology offices prescribe Repatha regularly and are usually familiar with both the copay card enrollment and the Patient Assistance Program application. Here is what you can ask them to do:

  • Enroll you in the REPATHA SUPPORT™ copay program on your behalf (they can do this during your visit).
  • Submit the prior authorization and handle any insurance appeals.
  • Complete the physician section of the Patient Assistance Program application if you are uninsured or underinsured.
  • Provide starter samples to keep you on therapy while paperwork is processed.

If your doctor's office is unfamiliar with the process, point them to repathasupport.com or the provider line at 1-844-737-2842.
